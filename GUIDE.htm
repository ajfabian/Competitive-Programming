Thoughts
- Trust me, it is all about:
    - Practice makes perfect
    - Step 1: Practice, Step 2: Go to step 1. No magic! 
- A lot of qualitative and quantitive factors will determine what you can achieve.
    - Some external factors are out of your hands (e.g. quality of your previous education)
- Training Quality (The training smart not hard dilemma)
    - Creating competitive environment around you
    - Practice as if you are in a contest (e.g. get AC 1st submission)
    - Learn the most possible from your mistakes, E.g.  Your bugs types & wrong decisions
    - Learn the most possible from others: editorials, solutions
    - Understand your mind thinking paths and how to improve
    - Avoiding bad habits
         - knowing some details about the problems before solving (category, level (e.g. in TC, CF))
         - Focus on specific judge or categories (avoiding what you hate)
         - Solving more in the wrong level (easy & medium vs the hard target level)
         - Acquiring much knowledge (breadth) on cost of mastering topics (depth)
         - Weak theoretical understanding for the algorithms (practice vs theory)
         - Just solving without attention to weak skills (e.g. weak debugging skills)
- Quantity: My claim, with every 400-500 hard problems, you should find a good change in your level.
 - I guess one needs to solve ~4000 hard problems to be one of the Ninja in the community
 - I am referring only to hard. Sure there will be other easy & medium problems to solve (e.g. to maintain speed, or avoid the underestimation/overestimation dilemma)
- It doesn't make sense to solve much less and still wish to be e.g. a strong red guy
- Online Contests and Upsolving. Participating regularly in ~2 contests (e.g. CF, AtCoder, CSAcademy) and upsolving as much as you could = a must for most of strong guys
- My role: In recent years, my belief in the importance of problem selection is increasing.
- I am not only trying to assign hard problems relative to your level, but doing my best to have them so interesting/original ideas
- This way, you will avoid solving hundreds of duplicate/similar ideas. Hopefully it means to solve much less while also still be strong ... also it will be more fun!
- On your side:
    - Be serious about training. You will reach no where by solving a few problems
    - Focus on problems I assign to you. You don't need to solve anything else.
    - I will also tell you when to learn what.
    - Communicate feedback when something is wrong. Don't just disappear. Try a few times to get what you wish/expect from this training
    - Online Contests & Upsolving

Problem Level column (VERY sensetive value)
Use values: 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, .... 9.5. You could also use X.25 and X.75
Level (1-1.5) = CF-Div2-A
Level (2-2.5) = CF-Div2-B
Level (3-4.5) = CF-Div2-C
Level (5-6) = CF-Div2-D
Level (6-7.5) = CF-Div2-E / CF-Div1-C
Level (7.5-8.5) = CF-Div1-D
Level (8.5-9.5) = CF-Div1-E
 
Problem level is not on idea level only (although this is the important part), but on implementation too. E.g. if you got the idea in 3 minutes, but needed 50 min to code it, this problem seems a hard one (e.g. maybe 5 or 6/10)
NOTICE: It is not subjective to your skills, BUT to codeforces itself. In other words, you could solve a problem in 12 minutes and it is so easy for you (e.g. solved a similar problem before), but you know this is a typical Div2-E problem, so possible problem level is 6.5/10 not 3/10

Here is an easy way to assign problem level:

1- Decide in a CF round, where this problem may appear. Say you think this could be Div2-E problem
2- From table, Div2-E range is 6-7.5 (6 for easy, 7.5 for hard)

Note, sometimes in real rounds problems themselves might be not as hard as expected from problem-setters. E.g. Div2-E problem was actually so easy (e.g. you assign value 5)
If you can't solve the problem, DON'T add problem level value.
Don't use problem level = 10. Always < 10
